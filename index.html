The HOPE Framework

Healing Opportunities for Processing Eco-grief

The HOPE Framework is a literacy strategy for identifying hope in narratives as a response to eco-grief. It helps readers find four markers of hope in stories about the environment: Motivation for Change, Belief that Change is Possible, Plans for a Path Forward, and Agency to Take Action.

Developed by Dr. Colleen Redmond, University of Minnesota. Published in Climate Literacy in Education, Vol. 2, No. 1, 2024.

Your Students Are Already Feeling This

Eco-grief — the sadness, anxiety, and sense of loss people feel about environmental destruction — is not a future problem. It's happening right now in your classroom. Students see the news, feel the heat, watch the wildfires, and wonder if anyone is doing anything. Without tools to process these emotions, eco-grief leads to disengagement, hopelessness, and avoidance.

But grief doesn't have to be the end of the story. When students learn to find hope in the narratives they read, they move from helplessness to agency. That's exactly what the HOPE Framework is designed to do.

🌿 The HOPE Framework

A literacy strategy for identifying hope in narratives as a response to eco-grief

Developed by Dr. Colleen Redmond at the University of Minnesota and published in Climate Literacy in Education (Vol. 2, No. 1, 2024), the HOPE Framework gives students a structured way to find hope when reading stories about the environment. Instead of stopping at the grief, students learn to look for four markers that show how people move from concern to action.

H
Healing — Motivation for Change

The emotional spark. Something in the story makes people want things to be different. This is where eco-grief transforms into purpose.

O
Opportunities — Belief that Change is Possible

Evidence that things can get better. Not blind optimism — real examples of success that build confidence change is achievable.

P
Processing — Plans for a Path Forward

Concrete steps and strategies. When students see characters making plans, they learn that problems have actionable solutions.

E
Eco-grief → Agency to Take Action

The shift from feeling to doing. Characters take real action — and students see that they can too.

Why This Works in the Classroom

  • Validates emotions — tells students it's okay to feel grief about the environment
  • Uses stories they're already reading — works with any climate-related text or literature
  • Builds critical thinking — students analyze text through a structured lens
  • Moves past helplessness — the four markers model the journey from grief to empowered action
  • Meets students where they are — age-appropriate prompts for elementary, middle, and high school

📊 HOPE Rubric — Elementary

Use this rubric to assess student work. Each HOPE marker is scored on a 3-point scale.

Marker ⭐ Emerging (1) ⭐⭐ Developing (2) ⭐⭐⭐ Strong (3)
🔥 Motivation Names something in nature they care about Explains why they care and how it makes them feel Connects their feelings to wanting to help or change something
✨ Belief Says things "can" get better Gives an example of something that got better Explains why they believe people can make a difference
🗺️ Plans Names one thing they could do Describes steps they would take Creates a simple plan with who, what, and when
💪 Agency Says "I can help" Describes something they already did or will do Shows ownership — explains their role and why it matters

📊 HOPE Rubric — Middle School

Use this rubric to assess student work. Each HOPE marker is scored on a 3-point scale.

Marker ⭐ Emerging (1) ⭐⭐ Developing (2) ⭐⭐⭐ Strong (3)
🔥 Motivation Identifies an environmental issue that concerns them Explains how the issue personally affects them or their community Connects personal eco-grief to a broader desire for systemic change
✨ Belief States that change is possible References a real example of environmental progress Analyzes why specific actions succeeded and applies that reasoning
🗺️ Plans Lists actions that could help Outlines a multi-step plan with specific actions Creates a realistic plan that considers obstacles and resources
💪 Agency Expresses willingness to act Describes concrete actions they have taken or will take Demonstrates leadership — organizes others, measures impact, adapts approach

📊 HOPE Rubric — High School

Use this rubric to assess student work. Each HOPE marker is scored on a 3-point scale.

Marker ⭐ Emerging (1) ⭐⭐ Developing (2) ⭐⭐⭐ Strong (3)
🔥 Motivation Acknowledges eco-grief or environmental concern Analyzes the relationship between personal experience, systemic injustice, and the desire for change Articulates a nuanced understanding of eco-grief as both individual and collective, connecting it to intersectional justice
✨ Belief Identifies reasons for optimism Evaluates evidence for and against the possibility of meaningful change Synthesizes multiple perspectives to build a complex, evidence-based argument for belief in change
🗺️ Plans Proposes actions or strategies Develops a detailed plan addressing root causes, stakeholders, and potential barriers Designs a strategic, multi-phase plan that accounts for power dynamics, coalition building, and measurable outcomes
💪 Agency Expresses personal commitment to action Demonstrates agency through leadership, organizing, or sustained engagement Models transformative agency — challenges systems, amplifies marginalized voices, and reflects critically on their own positionality

The Four Markers of Hope

When we read stories about the environment, we can look for these four signs of hope. Click each one to learn more.

🔥
H

Motivation for Change

Something in the story makes characters (or us) want things to be different.

Look for:

  • A character feeling upset about something in the environment
  • A moment that sparks a desire to do something
  • Words like "we need to," "I wish," or "something has to change"

Ask yourself: What made this character want to make a difference?

✨
O

Belief that Change is Possible

The story shows that things can actually get better.

Look for:

  • Examples of positive change that already happened
  • Characters who believe they can make a difference
  • Evidence that efforts are working

Ask yourself: What gives the characters (or you) hope that change can happen?

🗺️
P

Plans for a Path Forward

There are real steps or ideas for how to make things better.

Look for:

  • Specific actions characters plan to take
  • Strategies or solutions described in the text
  • Ideas for what could be done next

Ask yourself: What plan or pathway does this story suggest?

💪
E

Agency to Take Action

Characters or people actually do something — they take action.

Look for:

  • Characters who step up and act
  • Real people making a difference
  • Moments where someone moves from thinking to doing

Ask yourself: Who is taking action, and what are they doing?

    💬 Climate Conversations

    💬 Finding HOPE in Stories: Starting Climate Conversations

    Use the questions below to spark dialogue with students based on each of the HOPE markers. Feel free to add additional questions that are important for your students to discuss.
    Note: Markers often overlap each other and are not necessarily meant to be discussed in isolation.

    HOPE Marker #1
    Motivation for Change
    • What are the motivating events for the characters?
    • When do they first realize something needs to change? How do they react?
    • Does grief play a part in this story? If so, do the characters grieve alone or collectively?
    • Have students ever felt alone in their grief and anger about climate change?
    • How might talking about it with others help?
    • What role can understanding more about hope play?
    HOPE Marker #2
    Belief That Change is Possible
    • Where does the story show the characters believe they can make a difference?
    • What are times students have felt this way, too?
    • How do they feel they can make a difference now for the Earth?
    • What are resources available for creating collective climate action?
    HOPE Marker #3
    Plans for a Path Forward
    • What plans do characters create to solve their problems?
    • How does this show hope in the story?
    • Do the plans always go smoothly? If not, how do the characters react?
    • Resiliency in the face of challenge is a crucial element of hope.
    • Where have students felt resilient in their own lives?
    HOPE Marker #4
    Agency to Take Action
    • What actions do the characters take to create change? What are their sources of power?
    • What sources of power do students see in themselves?
    • How is power distributed across populations in regard to race, gender, socio-economic, or age groups?
    • How has this influenced climate change in the past and what are the current impacts?
    • What resources are available to help youth gain more agency and engage their hope through action?
    © Dr. Colleen Redmond, 2024 — HOPE Framework
